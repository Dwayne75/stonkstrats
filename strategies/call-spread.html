What You Need

  • 100 shares of SPY (or QQQ for a JEPQ clone) — about $50-60K at current prices
  • Options approval level 2+ (ability to sell spreads — most brokers: Schwab, Fidelity, IBKR)
  • 15 minutes every Friday afternoon
  • A basic understanding of limit orders

The Concept (Plain English)

You own shares of SPY. Every week, you sell someone the right to buy your shares at a higher price. They pay you cash (the "premium") for that right. If SPY doesn't reach that price by expiration, you keep the premium and your shares. Free money.

The catch: if SPY rallies past your sold strike, you'd owe the difference — that's where the "long call" leg comes in. You also buy a call at an even higher strike, which caps your maximum loss on the options side. This creates a "call spread."

Net result: you collect steady weekly income in exchange for capping your upside during big rally weeks. Over time, the income more than compensates for the capped gains — especially in choppy or flat markets.

TL;DR
You're renting out the upside of your shares each week. Most weeks, nobody claims it and you pocket the rent. When they do, you still keep the rent — you just miss some of the rally.

The Trade Shape

Example: SPY at $500 on Friday

You sell: 1x SPY $510 call (2% OTM)  → collect $3.50
You buy:  1x SPY $535 call (7% OTM)  → pay    $1.00
                                        ─────────────
Net credit received:                    $2.50 × 100 = $250

Both expire next Friday.

Important
Remember — you also own 100 shares of SPY. If SPY goes to $520, your shares gained $2,000. The spread lost $750. Net: you're still up $1,250 + $250 credit = $1,500. You only "lose" relative to buy-and-hold in massive rally weeks.

Friday Playbook

1

Check last week's spread

Log into your broker around 3:00-3:30 PM ET. Your spread from last week should have expired. If SPY stayed below your short strike, the full credit is yours. If it went above, the loss was automatically settled. Either way, the position is closed.

2

Note SPY's current price

Example: SPY is at $502.

3

Calculate your strikes

Short call: current price × 1.02 (2% above) = $512
Long call: current price × 1.09 (2% + 7% width) = $547
Round to the nearest available strike on the chain.

4

Open the spread

In your broker's options chain, select next Friday's expiration.

  • SELL 1x $512 call
  • BUY 1x $547 call

Enter this as a single "vertical spread" order. Set it as a limit order at the mid-price or slightly below.

5

Confirm and done

Your order fills. You see a credit hit your account (typically $150-$400 depending on volatility). Set a calendar reminder for next Friday. That's it — 15 minutes.

Common Questions

What if I forget a Friday?

No big deal. Your old spread expires and you just don't have one on for a week. You miss one week of premium but nothing bad happens.

What if SPY crashes?

Your shares lose value (same as buy-and-hold), but the spread expires worthless so you keep the premium. The income cushions the fall — in the backtest, max drawdown was -12% with 2% OTM vs -18% buy-and-hold.

What if SPY moons?

You participate in the rally up to your short strike. Above that, the spread costs you — but your shares are still gaining. You underperform buy-and-hold that week. Next week you sell a new spread at the higher price.

Can I do this in an IRA?

Yes. Most IRAs allow vertical spreads (level 2 options). The income stays tax-deferred.

How is this different from buying JEPI?

Same concept, but: (1) you pay zero management fees vs 0.35%/yr, (2) SPX options get better tax treatment (60% LTCG vs ordinary income), (3) you control the aggressiveness. JEPI is set-and-forget; this is 15 min/week.

What if I don't have $50K for 100 shares?

You can use a "poor man's" version: buy a deep ITM LEAPS call instead of shares, then sell weekly spreads against it. Same mechanics, less capital. Or just buy JEPI/JEPQ — the whole point of this strategy is DIY at scale.

Can I do this on QQQ instead?

Yes — same playbook, higher premiums (QQQ is more volatile), more income but wider swings. That's the JEPQ clone.

Key Findings

  • OTM distance is the dominant driver — spread width barely matters (±2% total return within each OTM tier)
  • 1% OTM: ~55% ann. yield, 3x buy-and-hold, but 27/101 weeks ITM (heavy upside cap). More aggressive than JEPI.
  • 2% OTM: ~26% ann. yield, ~2x buy-and-hold. The JEPI sweet spot — strong income with reasonable upside.
  • 3% OTM: ~10% ann. yield — matches actual JEPI distribution rate. Less income, more upside preservation.
  • 5% OTM: ~1.5% yield — barely worth the effort. Tracks buy-and-hold.
  • Max drawdown improves with tighter OTM (premium cushion): -8% at 1% OTM vs -18% buy-and-hold.
  • Tax advantage: SPX index options get Section 1256 (60% LTCG / 40% STCG) — saves ~$5K/yr on a $500K portfolio vs JEPI's ordinary income distributions.
